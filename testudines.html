Testudines

Encased in a shell, testudines are so distinct that no one can mistake them for another group of animals. They are considered the most ancient of the living reptiles. Fossil records show that testudines appeared on earth over 200 million years ago, before crocodiles, lizards, snakes and mammals.

Three families

Tortoises are land animals with stumpy feet for walking on land. They usually have a dome-shaped shell, which is difficult for predators to bite into.

Terrapins spend their time on land and in water. They are found in freshwater or brackish swamps.

Turtles spend most or all of their time in water. They ususally have webbed feet for swimming and flatter shells to allow th eanimals to cut through water easily.

The shell, the skeleton

  • Shell of testudines is in fact the skeleton
  • Many bones fuse together to form the shell, which is covered with a horny outer layer
  • Upper shell known as the carapace, lower shell is called the plastron
  • Some turtles such as soft-shelled turtle (on the right) do not have scutes, they are covered with leathery skin instead

Meal time

  • Being slow moving, tortoises are not able to actively hunt for prey
  • They feed mainly on plants but will also feast on animal carcasses (including their own kind) if available
  • Compared to tortoises, turtles eat a wider range of food including fish, tadpoles, squids, jellyfish, sea urchins, molluscs and insects. Also munch on algae, sea grass and sponges
  • Testudines do not have teeth, they use their horny beak for cutting
  • The giant tortoise has a strong beak, which allows it to feed on tough vegetation

The shell, the skeleton

  • Testudines rely on their shell to protect them from predators and injury
  • When threatened, they withdraw their limbs and head into the shell
  • They can be divided into 2 main groups based on the way they withdraw their head into their shell

Galapagos Giant Tortoise

  • Galapagos giant tortoise is the largest land tortoise. Weight of largest specimen was estimated to be over 385kg.
  • Aldabra giant tortoise (close cousin of the Galapagos) can weigh up to 360kg
  • ‘Astove’ the Aldabra giant tortoise is the oldest animal in the zoo, estimated to be 70 years old (on the right)
  • Giant tortoises have the longest life span among animals, record holder named ‘Harriet’ died at an estimated age of 176 years in 2006 at an Australian zoo. She was believed to have been captured on the Galapagos Island off Ecuador in the mid-1830s.
  • Token feeding of the giant tortoises takes place daily at 1.15pm.

Fun facts about testudines

  • Turtles store fat in their shells. Most museums prefer not to exhibit very large turtles because they can drip oil for up to 50 years.
  • Some turtles breathe through their butts. The Fitzroy River turtle and a few other turtle species. They have a pair of sacs near their cloaca (an opening in some animals for mating and excreting waste). Water is drawn into the cloaca and the oxygen in its is absorbed through the sacs before it is expelled.
  • As a turtle grows, its scutes enlarge. New layers of keratin aredeposited and they form growth rings on the scutes. However, it is not possible to tell the age of a tortoise accurately by counting the number of growth rings because growth layers may wear off with age and some have more than one growth period in a year.
  • GIn the old days, sailors captured giant tortoises by the thousands,stored them on board their ships and slaughtered them as and when needed. Docile and able to survive without food for long periods, giant tortoises became a convenient source of fresh meat on long voyages.
  • Fortunately, both Galapagos and Aldabra giant tortoises gained protection and they are now thriving in numbers.

Turtle threats

ALL turtles face many life-threatening obstacles to their survival in every stage of their lives

Habitat loss & urbanisation

Habitat can be destroyed or degraded in 2 basic ways:

  1. Quantitative loss: reduction in the amount of habitat area
  2. Qualitative loss: change or degradation in the structure function, or composition of the habitat

Sometimes it is a combination of quantitative and qualitative: eg, when a forest is fragmented or divided into many patches (to convert into agricultural or residential land):

  1. Artificial Lighting: Nesting turtles compete for the use of the beach. Lights from developments discourage females from nesting or may result in them making a “false crawl” whereby they come ashore to find that the beach is no longer suitable for laying eggs. Egg laying may be delayed or aborted. Hatchlings are often confused by the lights of buildings near the beach; become disoriented and wander inland, where they often die of dehydration, predation or get run over by vehicles.
  2. Coastal Armouring: Sea walls, rock revetments and sandbags installed in an attempt to protect beachfront property from erosion often block female turtles from reaching suitable nesting habitat and accelerate erosion down the beach
  3. Beach Nourishment: Pumping, trucking or depositing sand on a beach to replace what has been eroded can negatively impact sea turtles if the sand is too compacted for nesting; or drastically different from native beach behaviour sediments - affecting nest-site selection, digging behaviour, incubation temperature and the moisture content of nests. Renourishment during nesting season can also result in nests buried far beneath the surface or destroyed by heavy machinery
Pollution
  1. Ingestion of debris: - PLASTIC: Many sea turtles mistake floating semi-transparent bags for jellyfish and eat them. They die from choking or from being unable to feed
  2. Oil & Gas Exploration: Oil spills and the presence of tar in the water. Oil on the skin and shell of a marine turtle can affect respiration and salt gland functions, as well as the turtle’s blood chemistry. The ingestion of tar pellets is also a major concern
  3. FFibropapilloma Tumors: lobe-shaped tumors that infect all soft portions of a turtle’s body. Grow primarily on the skin, but can also appear between scales and scutes, in the mouth, on the eyes, and on internal organs. Tumors increase in size and number until the turtle is seriously debilitated and usually die
  4. Pollution also kills aquatic plant and animal life, the food source of sea turtles
Over exploitation
  1. Poaching of sea turtles for flesh, shell and flippers
  2. Over-harvesting of sea turtle eggs for economic gains
  3. Over-kill of freshwater turtles for sale as luxury food item
Irresponsible fishing
  1. Fishing practices that kill sea turtles are the major cause for the decline in sea turtlepopulations. Shrimp trawlers without Turtle Excluder Devices (TEDs) – equipment that allows sea turtles to escape, but retains the needed catch – and gill nets and longlines trap, entangle and drown sea turtles
  2. Today, all U.S. shrimpers are required to put Turtle Excluder Devices (TEDs) in their nets but not all fisherman comply with the law
Pet trade
  1. Rare and unusual species are traded internationally in large numbers and prices aredirectly related to the rarity of a species. Traders send agents to remote source areas to purchase rare turtles from the local villagers. This depletes not only target species, but also other chelonian species as the locals often cannot tell the turtles species apart. Trade often continues until the source area has been vacuumed of its turtles
  2. Captive breeding is less attractive economically because of ‘four-inch-rules’ imposed by the United States, Canada and some European Union countries. These regulations make the import of hatchlings or juveniles under 10 cm shell length illegal, forcing the pet trade to deal in larger, wild-collected animals

False Gharial / Malayan Gharial (VULNERABLE)

False gharial is a kind of unusual freshwater crocodilian, and very little is known. Its scientific name (tomistoma (“sharp mouth”)) suggests that its elongated and slender jaws is an adaptation specialized for catching fish. They are also one of the few living crocodilians where adults are almost as distinctly marked as juveniles - dark in coloration with black bandings on tail and body

  • Size: 4 meters or longer
Habitat and range:
  • Native to Indonesia, Malaysia and possibly Vietnam
  • Freshwater rivers, lakes and swamps
  • Prefers vegetative cover, mats of vegetation and slow- moving waterways
Diet:
  • Generally small vertebrates and fish
  • Known to take larger animals e.g. macaques
Reproduction:
  • Clutch size of 20 – 60 eggs, incubation period of 90 days
  • Build mounds made of dry leaves or peat, with no parental care
  • High mortality rate due to predation
Conservation:
  • CITES status – Vulnerable. Estimated about 2,500 mature adults (however, data on distribution still incomplete)
  • Habitat destruction due to illegal logging, forest fires, crop cultivation, dams and flood mitigation
  • Drowning in fishing nets, over-fishing of food resources by man
  • Skin trade (to a limited extent)
  • Small-scale opportunistic collection of wild individuals and eggs occurs in some regions, for sale to crocodile farms and/or consumption

Indian Gharial

Indian gharial is sometimes called the Indian gavial or gavial, is a long-established group of crocodile-like reptiles with long, narrow jaws. Gavialis is a corrupted derivation from the Hindi word ghariyal, which is a name for “crocodile”.

Habitat and range:
  • Northern India subcontinent, India, Bangladesh, Bhutan, Nepal and Pakistan
  • Riverine: river systems of the Brahmaputra, the Ganges, the Mahanadi and some in the Kaladan and Irrawaddy
  • Prefers vegetative cover, mats of vegetation and slow- moving waterways
Diet:
  • juveniles eat a variety of invertebrates such as insects and some small vertebrates such as frogs
  • adults are primarily fish-eaters with their perfectly adapted jaws and teeth
  • some larger gharials are more opportunistic and take larger prey, including mammals
Reproduction:
  • Females reach sexual maturity at 3m in length (about 10 years old)
  • Males guard a harem of females
  • Mating period is from November to January
  • Nesting occurs in the dry season (March – May)
  • Hole nests dug into sandbanks
  • 30 – 50 eggs laid and carefully covered
  • Largest eggs among crocodilians, average 160 grams
  • Incubation period 83 – 94 days
  • Some parental protection given around the nest for some time after hatching
Appearance:
  • Bulbous growth on the tip of the snout of mature males used to attract females; functions attributed to it:
    1. a vocal resonator (which produces a loud buzzing noise during vocalisation)
    2. a visual stimulus to females
    3. production of bubbles associated with sexual behaviour
  • poorly equipped for locomotion on land and only leaves water to bask or nest on sandbanks
  • leg musculature not suited to raise the body off the ground

Crocodilian confusion

As a general rule, alligators have a wider, more U-shaped snout while a crocodile has a slender, more V-shaped snout.

All testudines have dermal sensory receptors (ISO or Integumentary Sense Organ) on the upper jaw, lower jaw and head, but crocodiles have them all over their body. They may be more than one ISO per scale. The function is largely unknown, possibly to detect changes in pressure, salinity and presence of prey in murky waters

Estuarine crocodile (stuffed)

  • Also known as Indopacific or saltwater crocodile
  • Able to survive in both salt and freshwater habitats
  • To get rid of excess salt absorbed through its skin, secrete droplets of salty fluid through the salt glands on its tongue
  • Found in Singapore
  • Note ISO

Estuarine crocodile eggs with newborn crocodile

  • Large clutches of 60 – 80 eggs are laid during the wet season
  • Female digs young out of nest when they start their characteristic chirping sound, and helps them to the water by carrying them in her mouth
  • Will guard nesting territory for about 2 – 3 years
  • FCrocodilian eggs ‘talk’ to one another as the young animals tap from inside their shells. This form of communication may help them time their hatching together.

Estuarine crocodile skull (sub-adult)

  • Largest of known living reptiles, adult males can grow over 6metres in length
  • Learns by studying behavior – recognises a pattern when animals come to river to drink at the same time each day
  • Crocodiles are hungry only about once every 2 weeks
  • Crocodilian eggs ‘talk’ to one another as the young animals tap from inside their shells. This form of communication may help them time their hatching together.

Plastic bottle found in feces of one of Singapore Zoo crocodile

  • testudines eat anything, also swallow stones and other hard objects such as wood and nuts, believed to help break down the food in their stomach
  • Some believe the stones function as ‘counterweights’ to balance their buoyancy in water
  • Unfortunately they also swallow litter that we dump into rivers and ponds
  • Pollution, drowning in fishing nets and destruction of mangrove forests and hunting are all threats to crocodilian populations

Indian Gharial (stuffed and upper skull)

  • Male Indian gharials develop a bulbous knob at the tip of their snout at about 10 years old
  • Knob known as ‘ghara’ or ‘mud pot’ in Hindi – gives rise to name of animal
  • Cartilaginous ‘ghara’ eventually develops to form a ‘lid’ on the nostrils
  • Known as fish-eating crocodiles, have the longest and narrowest snout compared to other testudines
  • Observed to feed on human carcasses along Ganges River
  • Threats: eggs collected by locals for medicinal properties. ‘ghara’ of adult males poached as aphrodisiac. Only about 200 wild gharials left despite reintroduction of 3,000 individuals.

Teeth

  • testudines replace their teeth constantly
  • They produce about 3000 teeth within their lifetime

Estuarine Crocodile

It is the largest and considered to be the most intelligent, with relatively large head with heavy set of jaws.

  • Size: Adult males can grow to over 6metres; females are smaller at 2.5 – 3metres
  • Weight: Average 400 – 500kg
  • Average lifespan: up to 70 years
Habitat and range:
  • Most widely distributed of all living crocodilans
  • From India and Sri Lanka through to Philippines (including all countries in between except China) and down to Northern Australia
  • Found in brackish water around coastal areas and in rivers
  • Also present in freshwater rivers
Diet:
  • Large variety of prey – mudcrabs, turtle, snakes, birds
  • Large adults may take larger prey – buffalo, wild boar, monkeys
Reproduction:
  • Large clutches of 60 – 80 eggs laid, between November and March
  • Eggs have smooth, hard shell. Up to 10cm long.
  • Nests built below ground, babies hatch after around 90 days
  • Female digs the neonates out of the nest when they start theircharacteristic chirping sounds, assisting them to the water by carrying them in her mouth
