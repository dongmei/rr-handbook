Lizards

Lizards is the most successful group of all reptiles (almost 6000 species). They are making up about 60% of all reptilian species, and can be found everywhere in the world, except Arctic and Antarctic. Lizards typically have limbs and external ears. They also have 4 legs: some are large and powerful e.g. Iguana, while others are short and almost redundant.

Watch Those Eyes

  • Able to tell a lizard from a snake by looking at its eyes
  • Most lizards have eyelids that can be opened and closed
  • Snakes do not have eyelids
  • Exceptions - some lizards have a transparent scale that covers the eyes and others do not have eyelids at all

Legless Lizards

  • Some lizards have no legs
  • Adaptation to ‘swim’ their way through loose soil and burrow underground
  • Legless lizards often mistaken as snakes

Defence mechanism

As lizards are a source of food for many animals, they have developed many interesting ways to try and scare intruders away.

Change color
  • Skin of chameleon has several layers of color cells
  • Beneath these layers is another type of cell with tentacle-like arms (melanophores)
  • Color change caused by melanophores moving the color cells in and out of the upper layers of skin
Drop tail (Autotomy)
  • On tail bones of lizards are certain weak points called fracture planes
  • Tail breaks off at these weak points
  • Muscles are formed in such a way that they come apart easily
  • Blood vessels are constricted at the weak points, so do not bleed when tails disconnect
  • Replacement tail begins to grow immediately, but new tail will look different from original tail
  • This defense strategy is well developed in geckos, skinks, wall lizards, racerunners, whiptails and glass lizards
  • Biting, bashing, scratching - done as a last resort
Appear larger

Some lizards will puff out their throats to appear larger and more menacing, such as the bearded dragon, while others will inflate their bodies. The horned toad lizard inflates its body to the point that it appears like a spiked balloon.

Make surprise

The element of surprise is another method of defense. The blue-tongued skink sticks out its blue tongue and scares predators away by making them think it's poisonous.

Food and feeding

  • Majority are carnivorous, feeding on insects and other invertebrates
  • Usual strategy to catch prey – lie in wait, catch any prey within striking distance
  • Many lizards store food reserves in their tails
  • When there is shortage of food, tail shrinks as reserves gets used up

Gecko

  • Special lizards able to produce different sounds
  • Terms ‘gecko’ and ‘cicak’ (malay term for lizards) derived from calls of geckos
  • Generally active at night – eyes are large, often using their tonguesto wipe them clean
  • Most have no eyelids - eyelids have become fused and transparent (like snakes) and shed along with the rest of the skin
  • Excellent climbers; able to cling onto smooth, vertical and even over-hanging surfaces with help of elaborate ridges on the undersides of their pads
  • Toe pads are made up of special structures called lamellae, consisting of thousands of hair-like cells called ‘setae’. Cumulative surface energy from all the 14,000 setae acting together allow geckos to cling to smooth surfaces

Specimens on display

Adult Green Iguana (stuffed)

  • Able to grow up to 1.8m in length, largest species of iguana
  • Adults have crest of tooth-shaped scales along the back and part of tail
  • Tail has broad, dark bands around it and makes up two- thirds of body length. Used for counter-balance and defence
  • Large males develop a flap of skin (dewlap) under the chin (smaller in females)
  • Main threats - habitat destruction, pet industry and hunted for food
  • Native to Central and South America
  • Generally live near water and excellent swimmers

Green Iguana skull and jaws

  • Mainly herbivorous, small teeth for shredding plant matter such as leaves, flowers and fruit
  • Occasionally eat insects

Juvenile Clouded Monitor Lizard

  • Commonly seen in Singapore
  • Appearance similar to the common Water Monitor Lizard, but relatively smaller, about 1.5m
  • Distinguished by the position of nostrils being mid way between eyes and snout
  • Excellent tree-climber, with sharp claws
  • Monitor lizards feed on a huge variety of food types (eg. insects, crabs, snakes, fish, etc); they control the population of their prey,and in turn are a source of food to larger predators (eg. crocodiles)
  • important role as scavengers in biomass decomposition and recycling

Monitor Lizard lower jaws

  • Head is long with a pointed snout
  • Teeth are sharp and tips point slightly backward to hold onto prey tightly

Iguana Egg

  • Most lizards have eggs that has very little calcium in the shell – paper-like texture
  • Very little albumen present, developing embryo depend on water uptake from soil for survival
  • Female lizards seek out suitable nest sites with high soil moisture to supply eggs with sufficient water

Key exhibits on display

Komodo Dragon (VULNERABLE)

Komodo dragon are the largest living lizards in the world,. They are related to dinosaurs and ancestors are believed to have roamed Earth more than 300million years ago. It is difficult to tell male and female apart as they look similar – different scale patterns around genital opening unreliable. X-ray, endoscopy and DNA analysis are better methods.

  • Size: up to 3.1m in length and 116kg in weight
  • Life span: over 50 years
Habitat and range:
  • Exclusively in Indonesian islands of Komodo, Rinca, Padar, Flores and Gili Motang
  • Dry grasslands and hilly terrain of these islands
Diet:
  • Able to grow to a gigantic size due to voracious appetite
  • Can consume up to 80% of body weight in a single meal, equivalent to a 20kg child taking 16kg of food at one go
  • Throat and jaw muscles adapted to swallow huge chunks of meat quickly
  • Sharp teeth great for tearing flesh, and can have up to 4-5 sets of teeth in a lifetime
    • ,
  • Feeds on wild buffaloes, deer, goats, snakes and carcasses. Snacks on eggs, snakes, birds, rodents, fish, small lizards including baby komodo dragons
Shrewd hunters:
  • Flicks its long, forked tongue out constantly to ‘smell’ the air and locate potential prey or dead animals
  • Hunts by waiting motionless along a trail and ambushing unsuspecting prey
  • Able to sniff out food 11km away. Once a kill is made, all will join in to feast and fight over the best chunks of meat.
  • Can see objects as far as 300m away
  • Saliva contains at least 200 (?) different strains of bacteria. Once prey is bitten, unlikely to survive even if managed to escape as infection of wound caused by bacteria in the saliva.
  • Bacteria in saliva affect other animals only, komodo dragons immune to each other’s bite
  • Recent research into the feeding behaviour of the Komodo dragon has shown that it is actually venomous, possessing complex venom glands in its jaw, which excrete a variety of toxic substances that prevent blood clotting and lower blood pressure in its prey
Komodo dragons at Singapore Zoo:
  • On 15 Nov 2009, we welcomed the arrival of the first Komodo dragon hatchling in 34 years. The first zoo in Asia to breed them successful outside their native home in Indonesia
  • After 1 and half years of courtship, Yoko and Bima mated and a clutch of eggs was laid. In the wild, clutch size of 30 eggs are typical, and incubation period of 9 months
  • Yoko’s eggs were incubated artificially, 1 egg hatched successfully and the young was introduced into the exhibit in August 2010
  • More recently, a second successful hatchling on 1 June 2011
  • Vacuum assisted therapy (used for humans to heal chronic wounds) was used to heal Yoko’s wound sustained while laying eggs in a burrow
  • Unlike adults, baby komodo dragons are spotted (yellow speckles) and are agile climbers
Conservation:
  • IUCN status – Vulnerable. Fewer than 5,000 komodo dragons left in their native habitat
  • Regarded as one of Indonesia’s national treasures, can only be given away by the President
  • Increasing human population especially on Flores Island causing decline. Habitats being converted into villages and farms, also due to competition for food sources with villagers.
  • Fortunately, they are protected by the Indonesiangovernment.
    • ,
  • By breeding them, Singapore Zoo helps to ensure the future survival of the species
